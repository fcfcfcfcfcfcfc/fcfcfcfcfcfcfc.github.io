Rudin Chap.3 Exercise 6-10

发表于 2022-10-17 分类于 Principle of mathematical analysis
The problems on series

Exercise 6

Investigate the behavior (convergence or divergence) of \(\sum a_n\) if

  1. \(a_n=\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n}\);
  2. \(a_n=\dfrac{\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n}}{n}\);
  3. \(a_n=(\sqrt[n]{n}-1)^n\);
  4. \(a_n=\dfrac{1}{1+z^n}\) for complex values of \(z\).

Solution

  1. \(a_n=\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}+\sqrt{n}}\geq\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{n+1}}\geq 0\). By Theorem 3.28, \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}\) diverges. By comparison test, \(\sum a_n\) diverges.

  2. \(a_n=\dfrac{1}{n(\sqrt{n}+\sqrt{n+1})}\leq\dfrac{1}{2n\sqrt{n}}\). Since \(\dfrac{1}{2n\sqrt{n}}\) converges by theorem 3.28. By comparison test \(\sum a_n\) converges.

  3. Since \(\dfrac{3}{2}>1\), we have \(\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^n>\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^2>2\). And \(\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^n>n\) for \(n\ge 2\). We have \[ \sqrt[n]{n}-1<\dfrac{1}{2} \] which gives \(a_n<\dfrac{1}{2^n}\) for \(n\geq2\). Since \(\sum \dfrac{1}{2^n}\) converges. It follows from the comparison test that \(\sum_{n\geq 2} a_n\) converges. Hence \(\sum a_n\) converges because \(a_1=0\)

  4. The convergence of \(\sum a_n\) depends on the value of \(|z|\).

It is clear that if \(|z|<1\), then the triangle inequality implies that \(|1+z^n|\leq 1+|z|^n\) so that \[ |a_n|=\left|\dfrac{1}{1+z^n}\right|\geq\dfrac{1}{1+|z|^n}\geq \dfrac{1}{2} \] Thus \(\sum a_n\) diverges if \(|z|<1\).

On the other hand, if \(|z|>1\), then the triangle inequality implies that \(|z^n|\leq |1+z^n|+1\) so that \[ |a_n|=\dfrac{1}{|1+z^n|}\leq \dfrac{1}{|z|^n-1} \] Since \(|z|>1\), we have \(|z|=1+\delta\) for some \(\delta > 0\). Let \(N\) be the least positive integer such that \[ N\geq \dfrac{\log 2}{\log(1+\delta)} \] Then for all positive integers \(n>N\) we have \(|z|^n=(1+\delta)^n>2\) so that \[ |a_n|\leq\dfrac{1}{|z|^n-1}\leq\dfrac{2}{|z|^n} \] Hence it follows from comparison test that \(\sum a_n\) converges.

Exercise 7

Prove that the convergence of \(\sum a_n\) implies the convergence of \[ \sum \dfrac{\sqrt{a_n}}{n} \] if \(a_n\geq 0\).

Solution

Suppose \(a_n\geq 0\) for all positive integers \(n\) and \(\sum a_n\) converges. Since \(\left(a_n-\dfrac{1}{n}\right)^2\geq 0\), we have \[ \dfrac{\sqrt{a_n}}{n}\leq\dfrac{1}{2}\left(a_n+\dfrac{1}{n^2}\right) \] By theorem 3.28, \(\sum \dfrac{1}{n^2}\) converges. By the comparison test, we can show that \(\sum \dfrac{\sqrt{a_n}}{n}\) converges.

This completes the proof of the problem.

Exercise 8

If \(\sum a_n\) converges, and if \(\{b_n\}\) is monotonic and bounded, prove that \(\sum a_nb_n\) converges.

Solution

Suppose that \(b_n\) is increasing and bounded, then there exists a positive number \(M\) such that \(M\geq |b_n|\) for \(n=1,2,\dots\). Therefore we have \[ -M|a_n|\leq|a_n|b_n\leq M|a_n| \] Thus \[ |a_nb_n|\leq M|a_n| \] Since \(\sum a_n\) converges, \(\sum Ma_n\) converges. Thus \(\sum a_nb_n\) converges by comparison test.

Exercise 9

Find the radius of convergence of each of the following power series:

  1. \(\sum n^3z^n\);
  2. \(\sum \dfrac{2^n}{n!}z^n\);
  3. \(\sum \dfrac{2^n}{n^2}z^n\);
  4. \(\sum \dfrac{n^3}{3^n}z^n\)

Solution

  1. Since \(\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{n^3}=1\), we have \(\alpha=\lim_{n\to \infty}\sup \sqrt[n]{n^3}=1\), thus the radius of convergence equals to \(\dfrac{1}{\alpha}=1\).
  2. \(\lim_{n\to\infty}\sup\left|\dfrac{\frac{2^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}}{\frac{2^n}{n!}}\right|=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sup\dfrac{2}{n+1}=0\), thus \(R=+\infty\).
  3. \(\lim_{n\to\infty}\sup\left|\dfrac{2^{n+1}}{(n+1)^2}\cdot\dfrac{n^2}{2^n}\right|=\dfrac{2}{1+\frac{1}{n}+\frac{1}{n^2}}=2\). Thus \(R=\dfrac{1}{2}\).
  4. \(\lim_{n\to\infty}\sup\left|\dfrac{(n+1)^3}{3^{n+1}}\cdot\dfrac{3^n}{n^3}\right|=\dfrac{1}{3}\left(\dfrac{n+1}{n}\right)^3=\dfrac{1}{3}\). Thus \(R=3\).

Exercise 10

Suppose that the coefficients of the power series \(\sum a_nz^n\) are integers, infinitely many of which are distinct from \(0\). Prove that the radius of convergence is at most \(1\).

Solution

Let \(\{a_{n_k}\}\) be the subsequence of \(\{a_n\}\) such that \(a_{n_k}\not= 0\), we have \(\sqrt[n]{a_{n_k}}\geq 1\) so that \[ \alpha=\lim_{n\to\infty} \sup \sqrt[n]{a_{n}}\geq 1 \] Thus \(R\leq 1\).
