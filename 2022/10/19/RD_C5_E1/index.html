Rudin Chap.5 Differentiation

Exercise 1

Let \(f\) be defined for all real \(x\), and suppose that \[ |f(x)-f(y)|\leq(x-y)^2 \] for all real \(x\) and \(y\). Prove that \(f\) is constant.

Solution

We have \(-(x-y)^2\leq f(x)-f(y)\leq(x-y)^2\). If \(t>x\), then \(-(t-x)\leq\dfrac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}\leq t-x\). If \(t<x\), then \(t-x\leq\dfrac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}\leq-(t-x)\). By these, we have \[ -|t-x|\leq\dfrac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}\leq|t-x| \] Letting \(t\to x\), we have \(f'(x)=0\) for all real \(x\). Since \(f\) is constant.

Exercise 2

Suppose \(f'(x)>0\) in \((a.b)\). Prove that \(f\) is strictly increasing in \((a,b)\), and let \(g\) be its inverse function. Prove that \(g\) is differentiable, and that \[ g'(f(x))=\dfrac{1}{f'(x)} \]

Solution

\(\forall x\in(a,b)\), we have \[ f'(x)=\lim_{t\to x}\dfrac{f(t)-f(x)}{t-x}>0 \] If \(t<x\), then \(f(t)-f(x)<0\). If \(t >x\), then \(f(t)-f(x)>0\). These shows that \(f\) is strictly increasing in \((a,b)\).

Before we prove \(g\) is differentiable, we have to show that \(f\) is a one-to-one function,

\(\forall x_1,x_2\in(a,b)\), suppose \(x_1<x_2\). Because \(f\) is strictly increasing in \((a,b)\), we have \(f(x_1)<f(x_2)\). Since \(f(x_1)=f(x_2)\) if and only if \(x_1=x_2\).

Suppose that \(c\) and \(d\) are real numbers such that \(a<c<d<b\). Since \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\), \(f\) is continuous on \((a,b)\). Thus \(f\) is continuous on \([c,d]\). By theorem 4.17, the inverse function \(g:E\to[c,d]\) of \(f\) is well-defined and \[ g(f(x))=x \] Therefore, \(g\) is continuous on \(E\).

Let \(t,y\in E\) and \(t\neq y\), there exists \(s,x\in[c,d]\) such that \(f(s)=t,f(x)=y\). We have \[ \phi(t)=\dfrac{g(t)-g(y)}{t-y}=\dfrac{g(f(s))-g(f(x))}{f(s)-f(x)}=\dfrac{s-x}{f(s)-f(x)} \] Letting \(t\to y\), we have \(s\to x\) and \[ g'(f(x))=g'(y)=\lim_{t\to y}\phi(t)=\lim_{s\to x}\dfrac{s-x}{f(s)-f(x)}=\lim_{s\to x}\dfrac{1}{\frac{f(s)-f(x)}{s-x}}=\dfrac{1}{f'(x)} \]

Exercise 3

Suppose \(g\) is a real function on \(\mathbb R\), with bounded derivative (say \(|g'|\leq M\)). Fix \(\varepsilon>0\), and define \(f(x)=x+\varepsilon g(x)\). Prove that \(f\) is \(1-1\) if \(\varepsilon\) is small enough.

Solution

Since \(f\) is differentiable on \(\mathbb R\), we have \[ f'(x)=1+\varepsilon g'(x) \] Since \(g'(x)\) is bounded, if \(\varepsilon\) is small enough, we can make \(f'(x)=1+\varepsilon g'(x)>0\). By exercise 2, \(f\) is strictly increasing and then it is \(1-1\).

Exercise 4

If \[ \sum_{j=0}^{n}\dfrac{C_j}{j+1}=0, \] where \(C_0,\dots,C_n\) are real constants, prove that the equation \[ \sum_{j=0}^n C_jx^{j}=0 \] has at least one real root between \(0\) and \(1\).

Solution

Let \[ f(x)=C_0x+C_1\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\cdots+C_n\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} \] Then we have \(f\) is continuous on \([0,1]\) and differentiable on \((0,1)\). By the mean value theorem, there exists a point \(\alpha\in(0,1)\) such that \(f'(\alpha)\cdot(1-0)=f(1)-f(0)=0\). Note that \[ f'(x)=\sum_{j=0}^n C_jx^j \] The \(\alpha\) is the root satisfies the condition. This completes the proof.

Exercise 5

Suppose \(f\) is defined and differentiable for every \(x>0\), and \(f'(x)\to 0\) as \(x\to+\infty\). Put \(g(x)=f(x+1)-f(x)\). Prove that \(g(x)\to 0\) as \(x\to+\infty\).

Solution

Since \(f\) is differentiable on \((0,+\infty)\), we have \(f\) is continuous on \((0,+\infty)\). In particular, \(f\) is continuous on \([x,x+1]\) and is differentiable on \((x,x+1)\) for every \(x>0\). By the mean value theorem, there exists a point \(y\in(x,x+1)\) such that \[ f(x+1)-f(x)=(x+1-x)f'(y)=f'(y) \] Letting \(x\to +\infty\), then \(y\to+\infty\). We have \[ g(x)=f'(y)=0 \] This completes the proof.
